What BMI Doesn't Tell You

The real limits of body mass index — and what to track instead

Home › Blog › What BMI Doesn't Tell You

Published July 1, 2026 · 5 min read · BMI Basics

Your BMI — Body Mass Index — is a number calculated from your height and weight. It's the most widely used health screening metric on the planet, referenced in clinical guidelines from the World Health Organization, the CDC, and physicians worldwide. But despite its prevalence, BMI is an incomplete picture. Here's what that single number actually leaves out.

The Core Problem

BMI measures weight relative to height — nothing more. It cannot detect body fat percentage, where fat is stored, muscle mass, bone density, or metabolic health. Two people with identical BMIs can have dramatically different health profiles.

1. BMI Cannot Distinguish Muscle from Fat

This is the most well-known limitation. A 200-pound person with 12% body fat and a 200-pound person with 35% body fat will have the exact same BMI. A highly trained athlete — a linebacker, an Olympic sprinter, a competitive cyclist — often registers in the "overweight" or even "obese" range purely because dense muscle tissue adds weight. According to the WHO, BMI is intended as a population-level screening tool, not an individual diagnostic measure. It was never designed to differentiate muscle from adipose tissue.

~30%

Estimated proportion of people classified as "normal weight" by BMI who carry metabolically unhealthy levels of body fat — a phenomenon researchers call normal-weight obesity. (Reference: Romero-Corral A et al., European Heart Journal, 2010)

2. Where Fat Is Stored Matters More Than How Much

Visceral fat — the fat stored around internal organs deep in the abdomen — is strongly linked to cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and metabolic syndrome. Subcutaneous fat, the fat stored just under the skin at the hips, thighs, and buttocks, is far less dangerous metabolically. BMI captures neither. Two individuals with identical BMIs might have opposite fat-distribution profiles and, consequently, very different disease risk profiles. Waist circumference and waist-to-hip ratio are far better proxies for visceral fat burden than BMI alone.

3. Age Changes the Equation

As people age, body composition shifts. Muscle mass declines (a process called sarcopenia), while body fat typically increases — even when weight remains stable. An older adult with a "healthy" BMI of 22 may actually carry a higher percentage of body fat than a younger adult at the same BMI. Conversely, the WHO acknowledges that for older adults, a slightly higher BMI may be protective against frailty and fracture risk. The BMI scale does not adjust for age in adults, which limits its interpretive value for older populations.

75%

Percentage of U.S. adults over 60 who may have excess body fat despite a normal-weight BMI, due to age-related muscle loss. (Reference: WHO Global Health Observatory, Body Composition Data)

4. Ethnicity and Race Are Not Accounted For

The standard BMI thresholds — 18.5 for underweight, 25 for overweight, 30 for obese — were derived primarily from studies of European populations. Research has consistently shown that people of Asian descent carry significantly higher metabolic risk at lower BMI values. The WHO issued a consultation report recommending that action points for Asian populations be considered at BMI 23 for overweight and 27.5 for obesity. The same number means different things depending on ancestry.

5. BMI Ignores Metabolic Health Entirely

Blood glucose, blood pressure, triglyceride levels, HDL cholesterol, and insulin sensitivity are the actual markers that predict cardiovascular disease and diabetes risk. BMI correlates with some of these, but imperfectly. A person with a BMI of 27 might be metabolically healthy across every measurable marker. A person with a BMI of 22 might have pre-diabetes, elevated triglycerides, and hypertension. BMI tells you nothing about what's happening at the cellular level.

What Should You Track Instead?

  • Waist circumference: WHO guidelines flag risk at >88 cm (35 in) for women and >102 cm (40 in) for men.
  • Body fat percentage: Measured via DEXA scan, hydrostatic weighing, or bioelectrical impedance. Far more accurate than BMI for body composition.
  • Metabolic bloodwork: Fasting glucose, HbA1c, lipid panel. Ordered by your physician during routine checkups.
  • Blood pressure: A direct, independent cardiovascular risk marker that BMI does not capture.
  • Waist-to-height ratio: Emerging evidence suggests this simple ratio (waist ÷ height, ideally below 0.5) outperforms BMI as a predictor of cardiometabolic risk.

The Bottom Line

BMI remains a useful, fast, free, and reproducible screening tool. That's why it hasn't gone away. But it's a starting point — not a conclusion. A "normal" BMI doesn't mean you're healthy, and an "overweight" BMI doesn't mean you're not. Use it alongside other measurements, and let a qualified healthcare provider interpret your full picture.
